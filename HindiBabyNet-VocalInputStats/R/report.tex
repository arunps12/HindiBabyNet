\subsection{RQ1. Input Quantity and
Composition}\label{rq1.-input-quantity-and-composition}}

\textbf{Research question.} How does infants' vocal input, measured as
vocalization count per hour and vocalization duration per hour, vary
across input speaker types (adult female, adult male, and other child)?

\textbf{H1.} For both vocalization count per hour and vocalization
duration per hour, adult female speech and other child speech are
expected to contribute more to infants' vocal input than adult male
speech, based on Cristia et al.~(2023).

\textbf{Overall evidence category.} Weak evidence supporting the
hypothesis

\hypertarget{rq2.-role-of-individual-factors-on-input}{%
\subsection{RQ2. Role of Individual Factors on
Input}\label{rq2.-role-of-individual-factors-on-input}}

\textbf{Research question.} Does infants' vocal input, measured as
vocalization count per hour and vocalization duration per hour, vary
with infants' age, socioeconomic status (SES) of the family, and
infants' gender?

\textbf{H2.} Infants' vocal input is expected to increase with age for
both count-per-hour and duration-per-hour measures. Input from other
children is also expected to increase with age, based on Cristia et
al.~(2023). Higher SES, measured primarily using maternal education, may
be associated with greater adult speech input. No strong gender
differences are expected for input measures, although gender effects
will also be explored.

\hypertarget{rq3.-input-output-relationship}{%
\subsection{RQ3. Input-Output
Relationship}\label{rq3.-input-output-relationship}}

\textbf{Research question.} Is infants' vocal output, measured as
vocalization count per hour and vocalization duration per hour,
associated with the amount and composition (speaker type) of the vocal
input they receive?

\textbf{H3.} Infants' vocal output is expected to be positively
associated with vocal input. This association is expected to be
strongest for input from other children, based on Cristia et al.~(2023).

\textbf{Overall evidence category.} Inconclusive evidence

\hypertarget{modeling-note-for-family-2}{%
\subsubsection{Modeling Note for Family
2}\label{modeling-note-for-family-2}}

The Family 2 dataset contains three speaker-specific rows per
participant while repeating the same child-output outcome on each of
those rows. Including a participant random intercept in that structure
absorbed nearly all between-participant variation in the repeated
child-output measure, which produced flat input-output prediction curves
and unstable diagnostics. The final Family 2 models therefore retain a
location random intercept but omit the participant random intercept so
that the fixed input and input-by-speaker effects remain identifiable
and the fitted curves reflect the actual long-format data structure.

\hypertarget{rq4.-role-of-individual-factors-on-infants-output}{%
\subsection{RQ4. Role of Individual Factors on Infants'
Output}\label{rq4.-role-of-individual-factors-on-infants-output}}

\textbf{Research question.} Does infants' vocal production, measured as
vocalization count per hour and vocalization duration per hour, vary
with infants' age, family SES, and gender?

\textbf{H4.} Infants' vocal output is expected to increase with age for
both count-per-hour and duration-per-hour measures. Higher SES, measured
primarily using maternal education, may also be associated with greater
infant vocal output. Based on previous literature suggesting small
gender differences in early vocal and language development, female
infants may show slightly higher vocal output measures than male
infants, although findings across studies are mixed and effects are
expected to be relatively small (Rinaldi et al., 2023).

\textbf{Overall evidence category.} Inconclusive evidence

\hypertarget{modeling-note-for-family-2-1}{%
\subsubsection{Modeling Note for Family
2}\label{modeling-note-for-family-2-1}}

The Family 2 dataset contains three speaker-specific rows per
participant while repeating the same child-output outcome on each of
those rows. Including a participant random intercept in that structure
absorbed nearly all between-participant variation in the repeated
child-output measure, which produced flat input-output prediction curves
and unstable diagnostics. The final Family 2 models therefore retain a
location random intercept but omit the participant random intercept so
that the fixed input and input-by-speaker effects remain identifiable
and the fitted curves reflect the actual long-format data structure.
